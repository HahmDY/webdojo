Methamphetamine: Effects, Hazards & Prevention

Warning: This factsheet contains information about controlled substances for educational purposes only. The manufacture, distribution, or possession of methamphetamine is illegal and carries severe penalties.

What is Methamphetamine?

Methamphetamine is a powerful, highly addictive stimulant that affects the central nervous system. It takes the form of a white, odorless, bitter-tasting crystalline powder that easily dissolves in water or alcohol.

Methamphetamine is classified as a Schedule II stimulant by the Controlled Substances Act, which means it has a high potential for abuse and limited medical use. It is legally available only through a non-refillable prescription, though these uses are limited and rarely prescribed.

Methods of Use

Methamphetamine can be:

  • Smoked
  • Snorted
  • Injected
  • Orally ingested

Smoking or injecting methamphetamine delivers the drug very quickly to the brain, producing an immediate, intense euphoria. Because the pleasurable effects of smoked or injected methamphetamine disappear even before the drug concentration in the blood falls significantly, users often try to maintain the high by taking more of the drug, sometimes not sleeping or eating for several days.

Short-term Effects

Even small amounts of methamphetamine can cause harmful health effects, including:

  • Increased wakefulness and physical activity
  • Decreased appetite
  • Increased breathing rate, blood pressure, and body temperature
  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat
  • Hyperthermia (elevated body temperature)
  • Cardiovascular problems, including heart attack
  • Stroke
  • Seizures
  • Death from overdose

Long-term Health Consequences

Long-term methamphetamine abuse has many negative health consequences, including:

  • Severe dental problems ("meth mouth")
  • Weight loss
  • Skin sores from scratching
  • Anxiety
  • Confusion
  • Mood disturbances
  • Violent behavior
  • Paranoia
  • Visual and auditory hallucinations
  • Delusions (e.g., the sensation of insects crawling under the skin)

Brain Effects

Methamphetamine causes changes in the brain's dopamine system that are associated with reduced coordination and impaired verbal learning. Studies have shown severe structural and functional changes in areas of the brain associated with emotion and memory, which may account for many of the emotional and cognitive problems observed in chronic methamphetamine users.

Some of these brain changes may reverse after being off the drug for a year or more, but other changes may not recover even after a long period of abstinence.

Risk of Methamphetamine Production

Extreme Danger: The production of methamphetamine is extremely dangerous due to the highly toxic and flammable chemicals used. These chemicals can cause explosions, fires, and release toxic gases.

The production of methamphetamine creates toxic waste and byproducts that pose significant public health and environmental risks:

  • For every pound of methamphetamine produced, 5-6 pounds of toxic waste are created
  • Labs can contaminate the buildings where production occurs, making them uninhabitable
  • Surrounding soil and water can be polluted
  • Children found in meth labs are at particular risk for health problems
  • First responders and law enforcement face serious health risks when encountering labs

Legal Penalties

Federal penalties for the manufacture, distribution, or possession with intent to distribute methamphetamine are severe:

  • First Offense: 5-40 years imprisonment; if death or serious injury, 20 years to life; up to $5 million fine for individuals
  • Second Offense: 10 years to life imprisonment; if death or serious injury, life imprisonment; up to $8 million fine for individuals

Simple possession penalties (first offense) include up to 1 year imprisonment and a minimum $1,000 fine. Penalties increase significantly for subsequent offenses.

Treatment Options

While there are no medications specifically approved for methamphetamine use disorder, behavioral therapies have proven effective for many people:

  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)
  • Contingency management interventions
  • The Matrix Model - a comprehensive behavioral treatment approach
  • 12-Step facilitation therapy
